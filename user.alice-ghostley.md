## Overview

![Alice Ghostley](https://media.baselineresearch.com/images/98684/98684_full.jpg)

**Alice Margaret Ghostley** (1923-2007) was an American stage, film, and television actress whose gift was comic precision: the delayed blink, the worried smile, the little wobble in the voice that turned embarrassment into music. She won a Tony Award for *The Sign in Sidney Brustein's Window* and became a familiar television presence as Esmeralda on *Bewitched*, Cousin Alice on *Mayberry R.F.D.*, and Bernice Clifton on *Designing Women*.

## Bob Newhart

Ghostley's connection with [[user.bob-newhart]] was professional and stylistic more than biographical. They overlapped on Buck Henry's short-lived 1967 sitcom *Captain Nice*: Ghostley was a regular as Mrs. Nash, while Newhart guested in "One Rotten Apple" as Lloyd Larchmont, a nightclub owner who needs protection from potential killers. Their comic temperaments belonged to the same quiet register. Newhart made hesitation and bewilderment into a complete language; Ghostley made anxiety, propriety, and sudden alarm equally expressive.

That shared sensibility helps explain why both actors aged so well in television comedy. Neither needed to dominate a scene. Each could become funny by listening, pausing, and letting social discomfort ripen.

## Carol Burnett

Ghostley's relationship with [[user.carol-burnett]] was more direct. In the season 2 premiere of *The Carol Burnett Show*, she appeared with Jim Nabors and played Roger's sister Mimi in the recurring "Carol & Sis" sketch. The part placed her inside Burnett's ensemble world, where small reactions mattered as much as big entrances.

Burnett's comedy was elastic, theatrical, and emotionally open; Ghostley's was compact and exquisitely reactive. Together, they show two complementary kinds of timing: Burnett could lift a sketch into full comic weather, while Ghostley could change the air in a scene with a barely contained panic or a single delayed look.

> "Alice Ghostley was a master of the double take and the slow burn. She could say more with a twitch of her lip or a widen of her eyes than most actors could with a page of dialogue."
>
> Attributed to Carol Burnett, *In Such Good Company: Eleven Years of Laughter, Mayhem, and Fun in the Sandbox* (2016).
